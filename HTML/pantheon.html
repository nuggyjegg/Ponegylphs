PANTHEON

Temple of All Gods

The Pantheon is often called the Temple of All Gods, and later named Basilica di Santa Maria ad Martyres.

In Italian, it is known as Il Pantheon, and sometimes described as the Rotunda due to its circular design.

The Pantheon was completed around 126 AD under Emperor Hadrian.

Its dome is 43.3 meters wide, still the largest unreinforced concrete dome in the world.

The oculus at the center is 9 meters wide and is the building’s only source of light.

Renowned individuals such as Raphael and several Italian kings are buried inside.

HOW IT WAS BUILT

The Pantheon standing today is the third version of the structure, rebuilt by Emperor Hadrian after two earlier temples burned down. Although Hadrian designed much of it, he kept the original inscription honoring Agrippa, the first builder.

The Romans constructed the Pantheon using innovative engineering techniques, including progressively lighter concrete toward the top of the dome, hidden support chambers within the walls, and enormous granite columns transported from Egypt.

The building process used marble, stone, brick, and specialized concrete mixtures.

Granite columns weigh nearly 60 tons each, while the dome’s interior coffers helped reduce weight and increase stability.

Its open oculus was designed not just for illumination but as a symbolic eye to the heavens.

INSIDE THE MONUMENT

The moment you enter the Pantheon, you step into a vast circular chamber filled with soft light descending from the oculus.

The dome’s 28 evenly spaced coffers create the illusion of rising toward a celestial sphere, while the open oculus casts moving rings of light that glide across the marble like a slow-moving clock.

Raphael’s tomb rests inside, along with Italy’s kings Vittorio Emanuele II and Umberto I.

The interior design represents a perfectly balanced sphere, fitting flawlessly within the rotunda.

The floor contains hidden drainage holes to manage rainwater falling through the oculus.

THE STRUCTURE TODAY

Today, the Pantheon remains one of Rome’s most visited and most admired monuments, standing as a rare example of ancient architecture still intact.

It continues to function as an active church where Mass is celebrated, especially on significant feast days.

Despite earthquakes and urban changes, the Pantheon’s engineering genius has preserved it, allowing modern visitors to experience the building almost exactly as ancient Romans did.

It remains the best-preserved ancient structure in Rome.

Architects across centuries studied its perfect dome for inspiration.

The building still feels structurally solid due to the thickness and strength of its ancient concrete.

FUN FACTS

The oculus is often called the “Eye of the Pantheon,” designed to let in light, air, rain, and symbolism. During Pentecost, thousands of red rose petals are poured through it.

The Pantheon’s dome inspired the dome of Florence Cathedral.

It is the oldest building still in continuous use for nearly 1,900 years.

Rain falls directly inside, but the floor drains almost instantly due to the clever slope and drainage system.
