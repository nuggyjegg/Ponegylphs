TREVI FOUNTAIN

Fontana di Trevi

The Trevi Fountain is one of the most celebrated landmarks in Rome, known in Italian as Fontana di Trevi.

The fountain is dedicated to the Roman god Oceanus, who stands majestically at the center, surrounded by allegories of abundance, purity, and nature.

Known for its cascading water, sculpted figures, and luminous white Travertine stone, it has become a symbol of Rome’s beauty.

The fountain was completed in 1762 by architect Nicola Salvi and finished by Giuseppe Pannini.

It receives water from the ancient Aqua Virgo aqueduct, still functioning today.

HOW IT WAS BUILT

The Trevi Fountain took more than thirty years to construct, beginning in 1732 under architect Nicola Salvi after a competition held by Pope Clement XII. Its foundation rests on the end point of the Aqua Virgo.

Salvi designed the fountain as a dramatic, stage-like façade rising from the Palazzo Poli, blending sculpture, architecture, and waterworks into one unified masterpiece.

The materials used were primarily Travertine stone quarried from Tivoli, shaped into rushing waves and rocky cliffs. After Salvi’s death, Giuseppe Pannini completed the fountain.

The project used large blocks of Travertine transported by wagon, while the sculpture work required master carvers under Rome’s papal workshops.

The fountain merges architecture and hydraulic engineering in one design.

INSIDE THE MONUMENT

While the Trevi Fountain is not a building you enter, its “inner” features are found within its monumental façade. At the center stands Oceanus, commanding the flow of water.

To his sides are allegorical figures representing Health and Abundance, creating the illusion of a natural grotto emerging from the palace wall.

Beneath the surface lies a hidden collection system that circulates the water and gathers coins tossed by visitors.

The main basin is large enough to hold over 2,800 cubic meters of water.

Daily coin collections average 3,000 euros, donated to local charities.

The combination of sculptures and flowing water creates the illusion of a living stage.

THE STRUCTURE TODAY

Today, the Trevi Fountain remains one of Rome’s most visited and admired attractions, drawing millions each year.

The fountain underwent a major restoration completed in 2015, funded by the fashion house Fendi, which included cleaning and repairing stone carvings.

Its waters remain supplied by the Aqua Virgo aqueduct, continuing a flow maintained for more than two thousand years.

The site serves as a gathering point for tourists, photographers, and locals enchanted by the sound of cascading water.

The structure is monitored to prevent damage from crowds and environmental wear.

The Aqua Virgo aqueduct still brings fresh water from the countryside.

FUN FACTS

The Trevi Fountain is wrapped in stories and traditions. The famous practice of tossing a coin over the shoulder ensures a return to Rome.

The fountain has appeared in several iconic films, including “La Dolce Vita” and “Roman Holiday.”

An estimated 1.4 million euros are collected each year from coins alone, used to support food programs for people in need.

Tossing a coin with your right hand over your left shoulder is the classic tradition.

The fountain’s water is not potable, despite coming from an ancient aqueduct.
