BATHS OF CARACALLA

Thermae Antoninianae

The Baths of Caracalla stand as one of the grandest and preserved bath complexes of ancient Rome.

Built under Emperors Septimius Severus and Caracalla in the early third century.

At the center were the Caldarium, Tepidarium, and Frigidarium, three monumental bathing halls arranged along a central axis.

On both sides of the baths were the Palaestrae, open courtyards used for exercise and athletic training.

The complex could accommodate thousands of visitors, offering both leisure and cultural enrichment.

HOW IT WAS BUILT

The Baths of Caracalla were constructed during the early third century AD. Work began under Emperor Septimius Severus and was completed by his son, Caracalla, in 216 AD.

The massive structure was built using brick, concrete, marble, and granite. Concrete formed the structural core, allowing for vast interior spaces.

The heating system relied on a vast hypocaust network, where furnaces heated air that circulated through raised floors.

After completion, the baths covered more than twenty-five hectares and could host several thousand visitors at once.

INSIDE THE MONUMENT

The interior areas reveal the scale of ancient Rome’s greatest public bath complexes. Visitors can identify the main bathing rooms, including the tepidarium and caldarium.

These rooms were heated through an elaborate hypocaust system beneath the floors.

Beneath the baths, a network of underground service tunnels allowed workers to maintain the furnaces without being seen.

Fragments of mosaics and pieces of carved marble hint at the colorful decorations that once filled the building.

THE STRUCTURE TODAY

Today, the Baths of Caracalla stand as one of Rome’s most impressive surviving ancient structures.

Although the roofs have disappeared, the towering walls and vast arches still outline the original layout of the complex.

These remains help visitors understand how thousands of Romans once moved through the bathing rooms and communal spaces.

Ongoing conservation efforts protect the site’s surviving mosaics, underground passages, and monumental brickwork.

Visitors can walk across the expansive lawn where the natatio once stood and view the remains of the frigidarium.

The grounds are now used for outdoor events and concerts, giving new life to the space.

FUN FACTS

The Baths of Caracalla are one of the most iconic landmarks of ancient Rome. When they opened in 216 AD, they could accommodate more than six thousand visitors daily.

The complex covered about twenty five hectares, larger than many modern sports facilities.

Water was delivered daily through a special extension of the Aqua Marcia aqueduct.

The impressive heating system allowed the caldarium to reach warm temperatures even during winter.
