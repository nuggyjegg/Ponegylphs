COLOSSEUM

Flavian Amphitheatre

Colosseum, also known as the Flavian Amphitheatre, named after Flavian Dynasty of Emperors.

Located at the center of ancient Rome, near the Roman Forum.

It was built around 70–80 AD, under Emperor Vespasian, completed by his son Titus.

Made of travertine limestone, volcanic rock, and concrete and has the capacity of holding 50,000–80,000 spectators.

Mainly used for gladiator fights, animal hunts, public spectacles, executions, and even mock naval battles early on.

Considered as one of the Seven Wonders of the World.

HOW IT WAS BUILT

The Colosseum was a massive engineering project for its time, built through the labor of tens of thousands of slaves and skilled workers.

Each stone used in its construction was carefully numbered and lifted into place using cranes powered by pulleys and the strength of humans or animals.

The outer wall featured three levels of arches, each adorned with columns in different styles: Doric, Ionic, and Corinthian , stacked one above the other.

Inside, the Colosseum had 80 entrances designed for efficient crowd movement, much like a modern sports stadium with excellent crowd control.

INSIDE THE ARENA

Upon entering the Colosseum, visitors would notice the large oval arena at the center. The very spot where all the action once took place.

The floor that remains today is mostly gone, but it was originally made of wood and covered with sand. The Latin word harena, meaning sand, is actually the origin of the word “arena.”

Beneath that floor lay the hypogeum, a two-level underground complex filled with tunnels, cages, and elevators. This area housed animals, gladiators, and stage sets before they were lifted into the arena through trapdoors.

A complex system of pulleys and lifts, all operated by slaves, made these dramatic entrances possible. Wild animals such as lions, tigers, elephants, and bears were brought from all corners of the Roman Empire to battle and entertain the crowds above.

THE STRUCTURE TODAY

Much of the outer wall you see is damaged mainly from earthquakes and stone theft during the Middle Ages.

Some of the marble was taken and reused to build other Roman landmarks, like St. Peter’s Basilica in the Vatican.

Despite that, the Colosseum still stands strong after nearly 2,000 years, which shows how advanced Roman engineering was.

After the Roman Empire fell, the Colosseum went through a few phases.

It became a fortress, then later a Christian shrine.

In the Middle Ages, local families actually lived inside it and used the structure for housing and workshops.

Later, Popes restored it as a sacred site to honor Christian martyrs who were believed (though not historically proven) to have died here.

FUN FACTS

The Colosseum featured a remarkable retractable awning system known as the velarium, which was operated by Roman sailors to provide shade for the spectators.

Seating inside the amphitheater was arranged according to social class. The closer a person sat to the arena, the higher their status.

The emperor occupied a special royal box, while senators and the Vestal Virgins had their own reserved sections, reflecting the strict social hierarchy of ancient Rome.
