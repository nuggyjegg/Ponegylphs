CASTEL SANT ANGELO

Mausoleum of Hadrian

Castel Sant’Angelo, also known as the Mausoleum of Hadrian, is one of Rome’s most remarkable monuments.

Originally built as the burial place of the Roman Emperor Hadrian and his family.

Located on the right bank of the Tiber River, near Vatican City.

Its name comes from a legend in 590 AD, when Pope Gregory the Great saw the Archangel Michael sheathing his sword atop the mausoleum.

Originally commissioned by Emperor Hadrian around 123 AD.

Now functions as the Museo Nazionale di Castel Sant’Angelo.

HOW IT WAS BUILT

Construction of the Mausoleum of Hadrian began around 123 AD and was completed in 139 AD under Emperor Antoninus Pius.

The monument was designed as a massive cylindrical tower of stone and concrete, once covered in shining travertine marble and decorative bronze statues.

In the 5th century, it was incorporated into Rome’s defensive walls, and during the Middle Ages, the popes transformed it into a fortress and refuge.

Built using travertine, tufa, and Roman concrete, and later additions included towers, battlements, and papal apartments.

INSIDE THE MONUMENT

The interior of Castel Sant’Angelo reveals its many historical transformations. Visitors move through seven levels that trace the monument’s evolution.

At the base is the Hall of Urns, where Hadrian’s remains were once kept, accessed by a spiral ramp built during Roman times.

In the upper levels, the papal apartments display Renaissance frescoes, ornate ceilings, and marble floors.

At the very top, the Terrace of the Angel offers breathtaking panoramic views of Rome and the Vatican, dominated by the bronze statue of Archangel Michael.

THE STRUCTURE TODAY

Today, Castel Sant’Angelo stands as a museum and national monument, part of Italy’s rich cultural heritage.

It attracts millions of visitors yearly, who explore its halls, ancient ramparts, and the scenic terrace that overlooks the Tiber and St. Peter’s Basilica.

The structure’s unique blend of Roman, medieval, and Renaissance architecture showcases nearly two thousand years of continuous use.

Ongoing restoration ensures the preservation of its frescoes, artifacts, and fortifications.

The site hosts art exhibitions and night tours that bring the monument’s dramatic history to life under illuminated stone arches.

It is maintained by Italy’s Ministry of Culture and is connected to Vatican City via the Passetto di Borgo, still visible today.

FUN FACTS

Castel Sant’Angelo is wrapped in legends. The name comes from Pope Gregory the Great’s vision of the Archangel Michael ending a plague.

The Ponte Sant’Angelo, leading to the castle, is adorned with angel statues designed by Gian Lorenzo Bernini.

The final act of Puccini’s opera “Tosca” is set on the castle’s terrace.

The angel statue atop the fortress was replaced several times; the current bronze version was made in 1753.
