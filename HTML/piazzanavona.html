PIAZZA NAVONA

Stadium of Domitian

Piazza Navona is one of Rome’s most vibrant and historically rich public squares, known for its elegant Baroque architecture.

Built on the outline of the ancient Stadium of Domitian, preserving the shape of the arena where athletic games were once held.

At the heart of the square stands the Fontana dei Quattro Fiumi, designed by Gian Lorenzo Bernini in 1651.

It is also home to the Church of Sant’Agnese in Agone, begun by architect Francesco Borromini.

The square stands as a living showcase of Roman history, art, and daily life.

HOW IT WAS BUILT

Piazza Navona occupies the exact site of the Stadium of Domitian, an ancient Roman athletic arena built by Emperor Domitian in the 1st century AD.

The stadium was completed around 86 AD, measuring about 275 meters long and holding up to 30,000 spectators.

By the 17th century, under Pope Innocent X, the site was transformed into the magnificent Baroque piazza seen today.

The pope commissioned architects such as Gian Lorenzo Bernini and Francesco Borromini to redesign the square’s fountains and church façades.

INSIDE THE MONUMENT

The interior spaces connected to Piazza Navona reveal layers of history. Beneath the piazza lies the Stadium of Domitian, whose ancient brick corridors survive as underground galleries.

On the west side stands the Church of Sant’Agnese in Agone, displaying Borromini’s Baroque design with marble pilasters and a soaring dome.

The piazza’s fountains play an important role: the Fontana dei Quattro Fiumi features four river gods, while the Fontana del Moro presents a figure wrestling a dolphin.

At the northern end, the Fountain of Neptune shows the sea god battling marine creatures, creating a dramatic sense of movement.

THE STRUCTURE TODAY

Today, Piazza Navona stands as one of Rome’s most celebrated public squares and a masterpiece of Baroque urban design.

It attracts millions of visitors who come to admire its fountains, historic buildings, and lively cafés.

The piazza preserves the exact outline of the ancient Stadium of Domitian, allowing visitors to experience a public space active for nearly two thousand years.

The central Fontana dei Quattro Fiumi remains the square’s most iconic feature, rising beneath an Egyptian obelisk.

The Church of Sant’Agnese in Agone continues to serve both religious and artistic purposes.

Today, Piazza Navona is protected and promoted as part of Rome’s cultural heritage, under the supervision of the Comune di Roma.

FUN FACTS

Piazza Navona is one of Rome’s most enchanting public spaces. The name “Navona” evolved from the Latin word agones, referring to athletic games.

At the center stands the famous Fontana dei Quattro Fiumi (Fountain of the Four Rivers), designed by Bernini.

The church of Sant’Agnese in Agone marks the spot where Saint Agnes was believed to have been martyred.

Hidden beneath the modern surface are the impressive remains of Domitian’s Stadium, still accessible to visitors today.
